The Science-to-Headline Pipeline and Where It BreaksOn the structural distortions that occur between a research finding and its public representation

By the time a scientific finding appears in a general-audience headline, it has passed through a series of transformations — each individually explainable, collectively damaging — that typically leave the original result barely recognisable. This is not a problem of bad actors. It is a problem of misaligned incentives at every stage of the process.

The chain of transformation

A peer-reviewed finding travels roughly as follows before reaching a general audience:

  1. The research is conducted and submitted
  2. A university or institution issues a press release on publication
  3. Science journalists or general reporters cover the press release, usually without reading the paper
  4. Editors write headlines optimised for engagement
  5. The story is shared on social platforms, often stripped of its headline context
  6. The headline circulates as a received fact

Each of these stages involves incentives that push representation away from accuracy. Not through malice — through structure.

The press release problem

University press releases are the primary source for most science journalism. This is a feature, not a bug, from the university’s perspective: press releases exist to generate coverage, which generates prestige, which affects rankings and funding. The incentive is to produce material that generates coverage, not material that accurately represents the research.

The documented consequences are predictable. A 2014 study in the BMJ found that most exaggerations in science news could be traced directly to the press release rather than the journalist. Press releases overstated causal claims, overstated generalisability to humans, and omitted the probabilistic nature of findings at significantly higher rates than the papers they described.

The press office is not the villain of this story. It is doing exactly what it is structurally incentivised to do. The problem is the incentive structure.

The phrase "scientists discover" is doing enormous work in science journalism. Discovery implies finality. Most research findings are increments, contributions to an ongoing conversation, occasionally wrong. The grammar of discovery misrepresents the nature of science.

What reporters do with press releases

Science reporters at major publications typically have relevant training and read the primary research. General reporters — who write most of what most people read — do not, and often cannot. A technology journalist covering a metabolic study does not have the background to assess whether the sample size is adequate for the claimed generalisability, whether the finding has been independently replicated, or whether the authors are themselves cautious about the headline result.

This is not a critique of those reporters. It is a description of the knowledge constraints under which journalism operates.

The structural solution would be for all published research to come with a lay summary written by the researchers, specifying what the finding does and does not establish. Several journals have moved in this direction. It is not yet standard practice.

Headline optimisation against accuracy

Headlines are written to be clicked. The features of a headline that maximise clicks are not the same as the features that accurately represent the content.

A finding that suggests a link between X and Y, under specific conditions, in a small sample, becomes “X causes Y.” A correlation becomes a causal claim. A preliminary finding becomes a settled result. A qualified possibility becomes a recommendation.

**The three most common headline distortions:** 1. **Correlation to causation**: "X linked to Y" is weaker than "X raises Y risk" is weaker than "X causes Y." The first is often accurate; the third rarely is. 2. **Qualified to absolute**: "may reduce," "is associated with," and "preliminary evidence suggests" all become "does" or "is." 3. **Sample to population**: A finding from a specific sample (young adults in a US university psychology department) becomes a finding about humans.

The social media problem

Once a headline circulates on social platforms, it decouples from the article — and the article has already decoupled from the research. Studies of link-sharing on Twitter consistently show that a majority of people who share an article have not read it. They are sharing the headline.

The headline, already distorted, becomes the information people carry. Corrections rarely circulate with the reach of the original claim. The social incentive to share information that confirms existing beliefs outweighs the incentive to share corrections.

This is not a problem that can be solved at the level of individual behaviour, any more than the press release problem can. The individual science journalist who reads carefully, represents findings accurately, and writes hedged headlines is competing for attention against outlets that do none of these things.

What better coverage would look like

This is where the analysis is easier than the prescription.

Better science coverage would require: journalists with relevant domain expertise, or access to researchers who can provide rapid expert review; incentives for accuracy (reputation, correction culture) that are at least as strong as incentives for engagement; headline formats that accommodate uncertainty; and social platforms that reward accuracy as well as engagement.

None of these things is impossible. Some of them exist in isolated corners of the media ecosystem — certain specialist publications, certain science journalists, certain editors who care about this. They are not the default at scale, and making them the default would require changing incentive structures that are currently pulling in the other direction.

On the "study says" convention

The phrase “a study says” is a reliable signal that you are reading coverage that has not engaged with the primary research. Studies do not say things; they report findings, which have to be interpreted. The passive “a study says” launders the interpretive work that has been done (often poorly) between the data and the claim.

A note on the consumer side

There is a version of this critique that places the responsibility on readers: consume more carefully, seek out primary sources, treat headlines with scepticism. This advice is correct and mostly useless. Most people do not have the time or training to evaluate research directly, and requiring them to do so in order to form accurate beliefs about the world is an unreasonable demand.

The structural problem requires structural responses. Individual literacy helps at the margin. It does not substitute for an information infrastructure that is built to inform rather than to engage.
