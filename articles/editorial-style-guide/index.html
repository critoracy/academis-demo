Building an Editorial Style Guide for Independent PublicationsWhat to decide, what to leave open, and what most guides get wrong

A style guide is not a list of spelling preferences. It is a record of editorial decisions — what the publication believes about language, evidence, argument, and its obligations to readers. Most independent publications either have no guide at all or mistake a usage glossary for one.

Mara QuinnJanuary 8, 2026

What a style guide is actually for

Style guides have two distinct functions that are usually conflated. The first is consistency — ensuring that the same word is spelled the same way, that dates follow the same format, that measurements use the same system. This is the function most guides emphasise, and it is genuinely useful.

The second function is more important and less often served: editorial codification. A good style guide records what the publication has decided about contested questions — questions about evidence standards, the treatment of disputed claims, the use of anonymous sources, the relationship between criticism and fairness — in enough detail that editors and writers can apply those decisions consistently without relitigating them every time.

Without the second function, you have a copyediting checklist. That is not a style guide.

What to decide explicitly

**The ten decisions every publication needs to make:** 1. What standard of evidence is required for factual claims, and does this vary by claim type? 2. How are disputed empirical claims flagged — by whom, in what format? 3. Under what conditions are anonymous sources used, and who approves exceptions? 4. What is the publication's policy on corrections — speed, placement, transparency of what was changed? 5. When does a writer's personal position on a topic require disclosure? 6. How are conflicting expert views handled — false balance vs. genuine disagreement? 7. What is the policy on updating or removing published content? 8. What constitutes a reviewable conflict of interest for writers and editors? 9. How is opinion distinguished from analysis in the publication's taxonomy? 10. What are the publication's obligations to people who are written about?

These are not questions of house style in the conventional sense. But they determine the publication’s editorial character far more decisively than whether you use the Oxford comma.

What to leave open

Overconstrained style guides create more problems than they solve. If the guide specifies the correct way to handle every conceivable editorial situation, editors stop thinking and start pattern-matching, with predictably bad results in situations the guide did not anticipate.

A useful heuristic: specify decisions that have already been made inconsistently within the publication; leave open questions that require contextual judgement every time. The first category is genuinely helped by documentation; the second is hindered by it.

Building the guide: process matters

The style guide for the Independent Press Collective took three months to write and involved every full-time editor. This was not inefficiency; it was the point.

The process of writing the guide was itself an editorial calibration exercise. Disagreements that emerged in drafting — about evidence standards, about the treatment of expert consensus, about when statistical claims require caveats — revealed editorial inconsistencies that had been producing uneven content for two years without anyone quite noticing.

The value of writing a style guide is not only the document you produce. It is the arguments you have along the way.

Reviewing the result

The completed guide is available to publications under CC BY for adaptation. It runs to approximately 8,000 words — shorter than most corporate style guides, longer than most independent ones. The index is more useful than the running text for day-to-day reference.

Read the guide

The document is explicitly versioned: the publication date appears in the header, and the change log records what was updated and why. A style guide that was last updated three years ago is not a living document; it is an aspiration.
