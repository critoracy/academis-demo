Why Evidence Hierarchies Break Down in Policy DebatesOn the gap between what research shows and what policy accepts

The hierarchy of evidence — RCT at the top, expert opinion at the bottom — is a useful heuristic within clinical medicine. Applied uncritically to policy, it produces both overconfidence in what we know and systematic blindness to what we cannot measure.

Where the hierarchy comes from

The evidence hierarchy was developed in the context of clinical medicine, specifically for evaluating the effectiveness of interventions on individual patients. Randomised controlled trials sit at the top because random assignment eliminates selection bias — the most common distortion in medical research. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses sit higher still because they pool results across trials, reducing the influence of any single study’s noise.

This framework has been enormously productive in medicine. The problem is that it was then exported, partly deliberately and partly by imitation, to policy domains where its key assumptions do not hold.

What breaks down in policy contexts

The unit of analysis is different. Medical trials study individual patients. Policy interventions act on systems — cities, economies, institutions, cultures. The effects of a policy on a system are not the sum of its effects on individuals; they include emergent properties, second-order effects, and distributional consequences that experimental designs cannot capture.

The question is not whether evidence should inform policy. The question is what counts as evidence, and for what kinds of question.

The control condition is artificial. A true control group in a policy RCT requires that some population does not receive the intervention. This is often ethically problematic (if the intervention is beneficial), politically impossible (if the intervention is a legal requirement), or practically incoherent (if the intervention changes the context in which the control group operates).

Long time horizons. Many policy effects operate over decades. Education interventions, environmental regulations, and urban planning decisions have consequences that unfold over spans too long for conventional experimental designs.

Selection of measurable outcomes. RCT-style policy evaluation tends to focus on outcomes that can be quantified and measured within a manageable time window. This systematically underweights effects that are real but hard to measure — institutional trust, democratic participation, cultural change — and overweights those that produce clean statistics.

The LATE problem

Even well-designed policy RCTs estimate the Local Average Treatment Effect — the effect for the subset of the population that actually changed behaviour because of the intervention. This is often a small and non-representative fraction of the population the policy targets. The gap between LATE and Average Treatment Effect matters enormously for policy generalisation and is routinely ignored in media coverage of “what the trial found.”

The political economy of evidence hierarchies

Evidence hierarchies are not only epistemological. They are political. Declaring a certain type of evidence as the gold standard necessarily downgrades other types — including the situated knowledge of practitioners, the testimony of affected communities, and the accumulated experience of institutions that have tried things for decades.

This has practical consequences. In social policy, the communities most affected by an intervention are often those with the least access to the methods of evidence production that count as authoritative. Their knowledge enters the policy process, if at all, as anecdote.

A more useful framing

Rather than a single hierarchy, a more useful approach treats different types of evidence as appropriate to different types of questions:

- **Experimental evidence** is well-suited to questions about whether a specific, bounded intervention changed a specific, measurable outcome in a defined population. - **Observational and comparative evidence** is better suited to questions about how complex systems behave, how policies interact, and what unintended consequences look like. - **Qualitative and ethnographic evidence** is necessary for understanding mechanisms — *why* something works or fails, and for whom. - **Expert and practitioner knowledge** encodes pattern recognition that has not yet been formalised into quantitative studies. Dismissing it because it does not appear in a meta-analysis is a category error.

Conclusion

The case for evidence-based policy is correct, and important. The mistake is treating the evidence hierarchy as a device for producing certainty rather than for managing uncertainty. When it is used to declare knowledge settled in domains where it is not, it becomes a mechanism for closing off legitimate debate rather than informing it.

Scepticism about evidence hierarchies is not scepticism about evidence. It is a precondition for using evidence honestly.
