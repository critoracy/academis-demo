New Series: Cities, Data, and the Public Interest

A new editorial series exploring the intersection of urban infrastructure, digital governance, and civic imagination. The first two essays are live; three more are in preparation.

About the series

The Cities, Data, and the Public Interest series is edited by Thomas Vance and addresses a cluster of questions that have become harder to ignore as digital systems become structurally embedded in how cities function:

  • Who owns the data that cities generate?
  • When public infrastructure is built with private platforms, who controls the terms?
  • What does it mean for a city to have a commons in the digital era?

These are not primarily technical questions. They are political and philosophical questions with technical dimensions, and we intend to treat them that way.

Published so far

The first two essays in the series are now available:

  • The Unfinished Argument for Urban Density — on why pro-density politics keeps losing even when the evidence is strong
  • Transparency Requirements for Automated Decision Systems — on the accountability gaps in how public authorities use algorithmic tools

Coming up

Three further essays are in preparation:

  • The Municipal Commons: Digital Infrastructure as Public Good — Thomas Vance
  • Who Counts the City: On civic data collection and whose life it makes visible — a guest essay
  • Procurement as Policy: How public-sector buying decisions shape the technology stack of democracy — Thomas Vance

The series has no fixed endpoint. If you are working on something that fits, see our open call for contributors.
