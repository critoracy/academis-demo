subtitle: "Why historians distrust summaries more than anyone"

Historians have a professional suspicion of summaries that other disciplines have not yet developed at scale. It comes from experience: we have spent enough time in archives to know that the document described in a secondary source is often not the document in the box.

The summary flattens. It removes the hedging, the qualifications, the ambiguous phrasing that the original author included for good reason. It removes the context — who commissioned the document, who would read it, what they were not permitted to say. What remains is a confident-sounding paraphrase of a text that was never confident to begin with.

This is not a small problem. Many of the disputes in institutional history turn on exactly these stripped-away qualifications. A diplomatic dispatch that says "the minister *suggested* that the boundary *might* be reconsidered under *certain conditions*" becomes, in summary, "the minister proposed a boundary revision." The historian who builds on the summary builds on a misreading.

The lesson I draw from this is not that summaries are useless. It is that you need to know, for every summary you rely on, whether the person who wrote it had the same incentives you have. If they were writing for a general audience, they had an incentive to make things clear that were not clear. If they were writing quickly, they had an incentive to compress what should not be compressed. These incentives are not usually disclosed.
