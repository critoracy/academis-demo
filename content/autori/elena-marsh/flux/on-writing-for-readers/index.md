Academic writing has two audiences: the disciplinary jury that will judge the work, and the actual reader who wants to understand something. These audiences have different needs, and most academic writing is optimised entirely for the first at the expense of the second.

The disciplinary jury wants signals of competence: the right citations, the correct theoretical vocabulary, the appropriate hedging. It wants to see that you know the literature and are not making naive mistakes. These are legitimate requirements — scholarship requires a common language — but they are not the same as requirements for clarity.

The actual reader wants to follow an argument. They want to know what you are claiming and why. They want the evidence presented in the order that makes it easiest to evaluate. They do not want to hold three pending definitions in mind while waiting for the thesis to appear in the fourth paragraph.

My rule of thumb: if you cannot explain the core claim of a piece in two sentences without technical vocabulary, the problem is probably in the argument, not in the difficulty of the subject. The most complex ideas I have encountered in institutional history — about how power is legitimised, how trust is constructed and destroyed, how institutions survive discontinuity — can be stated plainly. The obscurity in academic writing is rarely the obscurity of the subject.

Writing for readers does not mean writing down. It means doing the additional work of making the structure visible, so that the reader can follow the reasoning rather than having to reconstruct it.
