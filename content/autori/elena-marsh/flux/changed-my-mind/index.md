**1. The value of bad first drafts.**

I used to try to write reasonably finished prose on the first pass. This turns out to be mostly inhibiting — the energy that should go into finding the argument goes into making the sentences acceptable. I now write the first draft badly on purpose. The resulting text is unreadable, but it is legible to me, and rewriting is much easier than writing.

**2. Statistical significance is a worse tool than I thought.**

I knew that p < 0.05 was arbitrary and that publication bias was a real problem. I did not quite understand, until reading more carefully this year, how systematically the entire evidence base in several social-science fields has been inflated by researcher degrees of freedom — the many small decisions that precede the statistical test and are usually not reported. The replication crisis is not an anomaly. It is what the incentive structures produced.

**3. The role of aesthetics in intellectual work.**

I used to think that caring about the form of an argument — its pacing, its prose, the arrangement of its sections — was a separate concern from its epistemic quality. I now think they are related in a way I cannot quite articulate. A poorly structured argument is harder to check for errors. A badly written one is harder to understand, and misunderstanding is a prerequisite for agreement without comprehension. The aesthetic is doing epistemic work, even if we do not usually describe it that way.
