"I know of no country in which there is so little independence of mind and real freedom of discussion as in America." — Tocqueville, *Democracy in America*, 1835.

Still the most uncomfortable observation in the democratic theory canon, because it identifies a failure mode that is entirely consistent with the formal machinery of democracy working correctly.

The point is not that majorities make bad decisions. It is that when social conformity is strong enough, the pressure toward majority opinion operates before deliberation rather than after it. People do not reason their way to the consensus view; they sense the consensus and avoid the cost of departing from it.

This is considerably harder to address than simple majoritarianism, because it has no institutional fix. You cannot legislate epistemic courage.
