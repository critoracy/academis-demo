Podcast fatigue is not about having too many podcasts to listen to. It is about the hour-long conversation format having colonised categories of content where it adds nothing and obscures quite a lot.

An edited monologue delivered in writing takes twenty minutes to read and can be processed faster than speech, searched, re-read, and shared as a quotable excerpt. The same argument delivered as a podcast requires eighty minutes, cannot be skimmed, and circulates as a recommendation to an already-overwhelmed queue.

The long-form conversation is the podcast format's genuine contribution. The slow accumulation of an argument across ninety minutes of dialogue, the moment when an interviewer asks the question they have been building towards since the twenty-minute mark, the kind of sustained intellectual encounter that has no written equivalent — these things are real and worth the time. There are perhaps three podcasts in any given year that do this consistently.

The rest are using an intimate, slow, attention-demanding format to deliver content that would be better as an eight-hundred-word essay. The essay takes less time to produce, less time to consume, and produces a shareable, citable, linkable object at the end.

The productivity content is the worst offender. But not by much.
