## The evidence in brief

The core claims are not seriously disputed in the urban economics literature. Dense urban areas:

- Emit significantly less carbon per capita than sprawling suburbs, primarily through transport mode shift and shared-wall heating efficiency
- Generate higher productivity through agglomeration effects — the density of human interaction in a city is not incidental to its economic output, it is a primary cause
- Cost less per resident to serve with infrastructure: roads, water, sewerage, and emergency services all have lower per-capita unit costs in denser configurations
- Correlate with lower rates of cardiovascular disease, partly through walking rates and partly through reduced air pollution from shorter vehicle trips

The debate is not about whether these effects are real. It is about who bears the costs of producing them.

{{< pull-quote autor="Jane Jacobs" >}}Cities have the capability of providing something for everybody, only because, and only when, they are created by everybody.{{< /pull-quote >}}

## Why the political argument fails anyway

The beneficiaries of density — people who will move there in the future, people currently priced out of expensive cities, the atmosphere — are diffuse, unorganised, and often not yet present at the planning meeting.

The people who oppose new density — existing homeowners worried about parking, light, character, and property values — are concentrated, highly motivated, and extremely present at the planning meeting.

This is a classic collective action failure, and good evidence does not solve collective action failures. Showing homeowners a study demonstrating that transit-oriented development improves regional housing affordability does not change the distribution of who attends Tuesday night zoning hearings.

{{< nerdnote title="The 'character' problem" color="warning" >}}
'Neighbourhood character' is the most durable objection in planning debates, and the hardest to engage. It is often a proxy for more specific concerns — scale, aesthetics, parking — but it is stated at a level of abstraction that makes it nearly impossible to directly refute. A planning application that scores well on every objective criterion can still fail on 'character'. Until this term has a procedural definition, it functions primarily as a veto for incumbents.
{{< /nerdnote >}}

## The distributional question that density advocates often skip

There is a legitimate concern embedded in density opposition that urbanists too readily dismiss: densification in already-expensive neighbourhoods frequently displaces the people it is supposed to help.

This is not an argument against density. It is an argument that density without concurrent affordability mechanisms can consolidate rather than distribute urban benefits. The communities most in need of accessible housing are not always those in a position to benefit from luxury mixed-use development, even if that development nominally increases overall supply.

A more robust pro-density argument acknowledges this rather than treating it as a cynical obstruction. The goal is not density as such. It is walkable, mixed-income, transit-accessible urban form.

## What better advocacy looks like

{{< box variant="highlight" >}}
Three concrete shifts in how density advocates make their case:

**1. Make the cost of inaction visible.** The status quo is not neutral — it is an active policy choice that produces sprawl, displacement, and carbon. Framing the choice as 'change vs. stability' allows opponents to occupy the stability ground falsely.

**2. Distinguish between types of density.** Six-storey courtyard buildings and fifty-storey glass towers are both 'dense' in planning parlance but have very different effects on street life, light, and community. Defending one is not defending the other.

**3. Address the displacement objection directly.** Density without affordability requirements is a real problem. Acknowledging it and proposing specific responses is more effective than treating it as a pretext.
{{< /box >}}

## Conclusion

The urbanist case for density is well-evidenced and morally serious. It fails politically not because the evidence is weak but because the political argument has been structurally misaligned with how these decisions are actually made — at the local level, by incumbents, under conditions that systematically favour the already-housed.

The evidence does not need to improve. The argument does.
