## The problem no one talks about

The dominant model of historical scholarship is built on direct engagement with primary sources. The historian goes to the archive, reads the documents, forms an interpretation. The authority of the argument rests, to a significant degree, on this direct encounter with the original material.

What this model does not adequately account for is how much of the work is done in translation — formally, where researchers rely on translated collections; informally, where researchers with some language competence read difficult texts with the help of dictionaries, colleagues, and intuition; and structurally, where an entire historiographical tradition about a culture is built on the work of a small number of scholars who actually had the relevant linguistic competence, and the rest inherit their interpretations.

I am not claiming that translation is always unreliable or that research dependent on translated sources is invalid. I am claiming that the degree to which historical claims rest on translation, and the degree of uncertainty that introduces, is systematically understated in the conventions of historical writing.

## What translation involves

Translation is not transcription. It is interpretation at the level of individual words, idioms, syntax, and cultural reference — and these interpretive choices compound through a text.

Consider what is lost in translating historical documents:

**Register**: the social and political weight carried by a choice of formal versus informal address, by the difference between two synonyms where one was used by courtiers and one by merchants, by a term that carried religious connotations in its original context that no longer attach to any available English equivalent.

**Ambiguity**: many historical texts are ambiguous in ways that were meaningful — ways that gave authors plausible deniability, or communicated different things to different audiences. Translation often resolves these ambiguities by forcing a choice that the original text deliberately avoided.

**Intertextuality**: texts written in conversation with a tradition of other texts rely on readers recognising allusions, inverted quotations, structural echoes. A translator who does not know the relevant tradition may produce an accurate word-for-word translation that has lost the point of the passage.

## The citation problem

When a historian cites a translated source, the footnote typically provides the title of the translated edition and perhaps the original language. What it rarely provides is any indication of which aspects of the passage being cited might be in question — whether the translation has been contested, whether the key term is a known interpretive difficulty, whether alternative translations exist and how they differ.

This is not dishonesty. It reflects the convention that the authority of a citation rests on the document, not on the translation of the document. The convention is coherent but it obscures real epistemic dependencies.

A practical example: the debate about the translation of the key terms in early Protestant theological documents has produced genuinely different accounts of what the reformers were arguing. Historians who have reproduced conclusions from one translation tradition without acknowledging the translation controversy have sometimes inherited contested interpretations as settled facts.

{{< nerdnote title="The Ottoman archive problem" color="info" >}}
Historical scholarship on the Ottoman Empire faces a specific version of this challenge. The relevant archives are in Ottoman Turkish, a form of the language that uses the Arabic script and incorporates large amounts of Arabic and Persian vocabulary. Very few historians working on Ottoman-influenced European history read Ottoman Turkish at a research level. The historiography is therefore substantially dependent on a small number of specialist scholars and translated documentary collections. This is not a scandal — it is a resource constraint. But it shapes what claims can be made with what confidence.
{{< /nerdnote >}}

## What methodologically honest historical writing would acknowledge

This is not a call for all historians to learn all languages — that is impossible. It is a call for more explicit acknowledgement of the linguistic dependencies that shape historical arguments.

In practice, this would mean:

**Flagging translation dependence explicitly**: where a key claim rests on a translated source, noting that the translation has been relied on and whether it is contested.

**Distinguishing between claims made in translation**: a claim about the argument of a document (which may be robust across translations) and a claim about specific wording or rhetorical register (which is much more sensitive to translation choice) warrant different degrees of confidence.

**Engaging with translation scholarship**: for many texts, there is a scholarly literature about the translation difficulties, the contested terms, the range of legitimate interpretations. Historians who rely on translated sources should engage with this literature rather than treating the translation as transparent.

**Using multiple translations where they exist**: comparing translations is a method. It does not require original-language competence to notice that two scholarly translators have made very different choices, and that the difference matters.

## Why this matters beyond accuracy

The acknowledgement of translation dependence is not just about epistemological hygiene in historical writing. It has implications for the authority of historical knowledge in public discourse.

Historical arguments are routinely invoked in political debate — to establish precedent, to characterise the nature of an institution or a tradition, to adjudicate between competing national narratives. When those arguments rest on translation dependencies that are not explicit, their authority in public debate is overstated.

This is particularly visible in the historiography of regions whose documentary record has been predominantly accessed by outside scholars in translation — the Ottoman Empire, imperial China, the pre-colonial African kingdoms. The degree to which the historical understanding of these regions is shaped by the interpretive choices of a relatively small number of translators and their institutional contexts is under-examined relative to its significance.

## Conclusion

The epistemological situation of the historian who works with translated sources is not fundamentally different from the epistemological situation of the historian who works with any kind of imperfect evidence. All primary sources are partial, positioned, and require interpretation. Translation adds another layer of interpretive dependency.

The appropriate response is not to refuse to use translated sources or to refuse to make claims based on them. It is to be explicit about the dependency, honest about the uncertainty it introduces, and precise about which claims are robust across translation variants and which are not.

Historical writing that does this well is harder to write and less satisfying to read in the short term. It is also more likely to be right.
