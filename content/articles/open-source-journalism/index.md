## Why this existed

The Northgate industrial district was built between 1930 and 1960 to house light manufacturing — machine shops, print works, small-batch fabrication. By 1990, most of the original industrial use had left. By 2000, the buildings had been informally subdivided, extended, and repurposed by a range of occupants: artist studios, small workshops, storage, light commercial, a few live-work conversions that technically violated planning conditions.

This kind of fabric is architecturally unremarkable and historically important. It is the physical record of how a post-industrial city absorbs economic change — how buildings that were designed for one purpose adapt, incrementally, to serve a dozen others. It is also acutely vulnerable. When a district is designated for redevelopment, this kind of fine-grained informal adaptation is the first thing to disappear, because it does not conform to any listed-building criteria and generates no formal planning record.

The Northgate archive project started when three buildings were demolished without any documentation being made. The fourth was scheduled for demolition in eight months.

## What we built

The archive is a structured, searchable, openly licensed documentary record. It contains:

- **Measured floor plans** of 34 buildings (11,200 sq metres of floor area), drawn from physical survey and cross-referenced against available historic plans where they existed
- **Photographic documentation** — 4,800 images following a consistent protocol: exterior elevations, interior condition, evidence of informal modification, detail shots of material junctions
- **Occupancy histories** compiled from planning records, rate books, trade directories, and oral histories with 23 former occupants
- **Material schedules** recording the construction materials and modification types present in each building

## Methodological choices

The documentation protocol was designed to be reproducible by teams without specialist architectural training. We wanted the methodology to be adoptable, not just the archive.

Several choices shaped the project:

**Consistency over comprehensiveness**: the photographic protocol specifies exactly which views must be recorded for each building type. This produces less visually interesting material than an unconstrained approach, and much more useful comparative data.

**Oral history as structural evidence**: the occupancy histories are not decorative context. They are evidence of how spaces were used, modified, and understood by the people who worked in them — evidence that is invisible in the physical record.

**Open licensing from the start**: all material was licensed CC BY 4.0 from day one, which affected how we obtained consent from oral history participants and how we negotiated access with building owners. It also meant the archive was usable immediately by researchers and practitioners without requesting permission.

## What we learned about informal adaptation

The most consistent finding was the degree to which informal modification tracks economic pressure. Buildings subdivided most heavily during the 1990s recession; buildings that remained single-occupancy tended to have had stable, well-capitalised tenants throughout. The physical fabric is a direct record of economic conditions.

The second finding was the sophistication of the informal modifications. Subdivisions were done cheaply but not carelessly. Occupants developed solutions — for access, ventilation, acoustic separation — that were tailored to specific conditions of each building. The informal modifications were better adapted to actual use patterns than many formally designed conversions we had seen elsewhere.

This has direct implications for how planning policy treats informal building adaptation. The current framework treats modification without permission as a regulatory failure. The archive suggests it might more accurately be treated as a distributed design resource that planning policy has not yet learned to see.
