## The privatisation default

When a city government needs new digital infrastructure — broadband connectivity, a smart-sensor network, a civic data platform — the default procurement model is to find a private vendor, agree a service contract, and accept the terms on offer. The vendor builds the system, maintains it, and retains ownership of both the infrastructure and the data it generates. The city gains a service; the vendor gains a perpetual asset.

This model has a coherent logic. The private sector has technical expertise the public sector lacks. The capital investment is off the city's balance sheet. Procurement is faster and cheaper than building in-house capacity.

It also has a coherent set of consequences that most city governments systematically underestimate at the point of procurement. The city becomes dependent on a vendor it cannot easily exit. The data generated about public life is owned by a private entity that may sell it, repurpose it, or restrict access to it. The infrastructure that underpins civic services is subject to commercial decisions made elsewhere — price changes, product discontinuations, acquisitions, bankruptcies.

These are not hypothetical risks. They are documented outcomes of a procurement model that has been the default for two decades.

## What a commons model would look like

The argument for treating digital civic infrastructure as a commons is not primarily ideological. It follows from the analysis of what digital infrastructure actually is.

Digital infrastructure in a city context is not like other goods the city might procure. A cleaning contract delivers a service; when the contract ends, no permanent dependency is created. A data platform, a sensor network, or a connectivity layer is different: it becomes embedded in civic operations, accumulates data that has increasing value over time, and creates switching costs that rise with every year of use. It behaves more like the water mains than like the street cleaning.

{{< pull-quote >}}
The city that cannot access its own civic data because a vendor contract has lapsed is not a city with a procurement problem. It is a city that has surrendered a public resource to private control.
{{< /pull-quote >}}

A commons model for digital infrastructure has several defining characteristics:

**Public ownership of the infrastructure layer**: the physical and logical infrastructure (fibre, compute capacity, data storage) is owned by the city or a public trust, not a private vendor. The services that run on top of it can be delivered by private providers under contracts with clear data-ownership and access terms.

**Open data by default**: data generated by publicly owned infrastructure is treated as a public resource, subject to appropriate privacy protections. It is accessible to researchers, civil society, and other public bodies without commercial restriction.

**Open standards and interoperability**: systems are built on open standards so that the city is not locked into a vendor's proprietary architecture. This is technically unremarkable — open standards have been the foundation of the internet for fifty years — but it is routinely sacrificed at the altar of procurement convenience.

## The Barcelona case

Barcelona's superblocks initiative is the urban design example everyone cites. Less cited is Barcelona's attempt, during the same period, to develop a municipal data commons — a framework for treating civic data as a shared resource rather than a procurement by-product.

The *Directriu de Sobirania Tecnològica i de Dades* (2018) was the most serious attempt by any European city to specify what data sovereignty would actually require in procurement terms. It mandated open standards, required data portability, restricted data-sharing with third parties without consent, and established default public ownership of civic data.

The results were mixed. Several large procurement decisions were made on different terms anyway. The political will to enforce the framework against well-resourced vendors proved harder to sustain than the political will to write it.

But the Barcelona case is instructive for the right reasons. It demonstrated that a municipality could specify a data commons framework in practical procurement terms. The obstacles were not technical or legal; they were institutional capacity and political staying power.

{{< nerdnote title="The Sidewalk Toronto comparison" color="warning" >}}
The Sidewalk Labs Toronto project — Alphabet's attempt to build a data-intensive "smart neighbourhood" on publicly owned land — ended in 2020 after sustained public opposition. The specific concerns were about data governance: who would own, control, and benefit from the data generated by a dense urban sensor network. The project's failure does not mean the technology was wrong. It means the governance model was not acceptable to the people who would have lived under it. That is useful information.
{{< /nerdnote >}}

## What it would actually require

A municipal commons model for digital infrastructure requires three things that are currently scarce in local government.

**Technical capacity inside the institution**: you cannot specify or enforce data sovereignty terms in procurement if you do not have technical staff who understand what those terms mean in practice. Most local authorities do not. Building this capacity requires sustained investment in recruitment and retention of technical expertise at salary levels that compete with the private sector — which means either paying more or making public-sector technical work more attractive on non-salary dimensions.

**Political will to exit bad contracts**: the sunk-cost problem in public-sector technology is severe. Systems that are not performing as specified, and that violate the spirit of the data governance framework, are routinely maintained because exit is expensive and disruptive. A commons model requires the institutional willingness to bear those costs.

**Shared infrastructure at scale**: the economics of public digital infrastructure improve with scale. A single municipality building its own broadband network faces high fixed costs relative to its user base. A shared infrastructure trust — serving multiple municipalities, sharing capital costs and technical capacity — faces much better economics. Several European countries have begun building these structures; the UK and the US are significantly behind.

## The political argument

The case for the municipal commons is not purely a case about efficiency or even about governance. It is also a case about what cities are for.

Cities are the most successful institutions human beings have developed for collective life. The density that makes cities productive also makes them sites of shared infrastructure: roads, water, energy, schools. The argument for including digital infrastructure in this list is not that the public sector always builds things better — it often does not. It is that some infrastructure is too constitutive of civic life to leave its governance entirely to commercial logic.

The political argument for the municipal commons is the same argument that justified public ownership of the water mains in the nineteenth century. The technology is different. The structural logic is the same.
