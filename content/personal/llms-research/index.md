After eight months of deliberate, systematic use of language models as research tools — not for writing, but for the preceding stages — a provisional verdict.

**Genuinely useful:**
- Explaining the structure of a field I am entering from outside
- Generating a first bibliography to pressure-test against actual databases
- Identifying the names of debates and frameworks so I can search for the right terms
- Drafting the skeleton of an argument structure from an outline

**Not useful:**
- Checking citations — the hallucination rate on specific facts is still high enough that I verify everything anyway, which negates the time saving
- Anything requiring access to my actual sources — the context window approach of pasting in documents works but is cumbersome at scale
- Evaluating the quality of arguments — it is good at identifying structure but not at identifying whether the premises are actually true

The most common mistake I see in how people use these tools is treating a confident-sounding answer as a reliable answer. The confidence and the reliability are decoupled. This is a significant learning curve, especially for people whose mental model of search is "find the right result" rather than "identify what I still need to verify."

I will revisit this in another eight months.
