Paywalls are not a content strategy. They are a funding strategy that has content consequences.

The criticism of paywalls — that they restrict access to information — is correct but incomplete. The more significant effect is what they do to the type of content that gets produced. When revenue depends on subscriptions, you produce content that generates subscription value: exclusivity, depth, a reader identity, a reason to pay. This is not the same set of incentives as producing content with the highest public value.

Public-value content — explanatory journalism, accountability reporting, analysis of decisions that affect people who cannot pay — is systematically underproduced in a subscription model because the people who most need it are frequently the people least able to pay for it.

This is not an argument against charging for good journalism. It is an argument that subscriptions should not be the only funding model for journalism, and that treating paywalls as a complete solution to the funding problem misidentifies what the problem actually is.

The publications doing the most interesting work on alternative models — grant funding, endowment, reader patronage at lower price points, institutional subscriptions with open access — are not getting enough attention from the people who write about the media industry, possibly because those publications are also competitors.
