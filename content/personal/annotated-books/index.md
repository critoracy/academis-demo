There is something unusually intimate about finding a book whose previous owner has marked it more heavily than you would.

I picked up a secondhand copy of a Stuart Hall essay collection last month. The previous reader — no name, just a handwriting style — had underlined heavily in the first three essays, sporadically in the middle, and not at all from page 200 onwards. Either they stopped reading or they stopped arguing with the text. I suspect the latter. The annotations are combative, slightly irritable, and then suddenly nothing.

I find myself reading their marginalia before I read the text. Sometimes this is useful — they caught things I would have missed, flagged contradictions I am glad to know about in advance. Sometimes it is a distraction, like trying to think through someone else's disagreement instead of your own.

The best annotated books I own are those where the previous reader was more expert than I am in the relevant area. Their marginal notes perform the function of a seminar: they tell me what the argument looks like from inside a specific field, which is not always what it looks like from outside it.

The worst are the ones where someone has underlined inspirational passages. That is not annotation. That is decorating.
