The Summer Book is composed of short chapters that function like observations rather than episodes. A grandmother and her granddaughter on a small Finnish island. The child's father exists at the edges of the book; the grandmother's age is not given but present in everything she does. The novel is not about plot. It is about attention — the kind directed at things that are small, finite, and not usually considered worth writing down.

Jansson is doing something unusual with the grandmother-granddaughter relationship. It is not warm in the expected sense. It is honest in a way that requires the grandmother to take the child seriously as a person whose reasoning matters, even when the reasoning is about magic or injustice or the correct way to build a house for worms. The mutual respect is never stated. It is evident in every exchange.

The chapters where things go wrong — the boat that capsizes, the disagreement that ends in both of them going to bed angry, the cat that dies — are not dramatic. They are treated at the same temperature as the chapters where nothing happens at all. This is formally striking and emotionally very precise. The effect is of a life in which all of it matters equally, which is not how novels usually work but is closer to how experience actually functions.

I read it in three hours on a Sunday afternoon in January, which was the wrong season and possibly the right one.
