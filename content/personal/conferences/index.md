---
title: "Conferences are mostly a waste of time"
publishDate: 2026-04-28
lead: "Not all conferences. But the value is concentrated in a few hours of the programme, and you generally cannot predict in advance which hours."
tags: ["academia", "opinion"]
---

Not all conferences. But the useful parts of any conference I have attended could have been scheduled into about four hours, if anyone had known in advance which four hours those were.

The problem is that the useful parts are not the programme. They are the conversations between the sessions, the dinner where someone mentions a source you had not heard of, the question-and-answer exchange that turns into a standing argument in the corridor afterwards. These cannot be scheduled because they are emergent — they depend on a specific configuration of people and questions that you cannot assemble artificially.

The programme is the pretext. The conference is the surplus.

This suggests a different approach: go to conferences for the people, not for the papers. Read the papers before you go. Make a list of the three people you most want to talk to and treat finding a natural moment to talk to them as the primary objective. Everything else is overhead.

I have mostly followed this approach for the past four years and it has significantly improved my conference experience, at the cost of attending fewer panels and feeling vaguely guilty about it throughout.
