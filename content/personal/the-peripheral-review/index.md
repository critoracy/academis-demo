Gibson solved the time-travel paradox problem by making the past genuinely inaccessible: you can send information back, and information can interact with the past, but you have branched into a different timeline. The original future is not affected. The people you are helping exist in a stub that was created by your contact.

This is elegant for several reasons. It eliminates the grandfather paradox while keeping the emotional stakes — you are not saving your own past, you are saving someone else's present. It also allows the novel to be genuinely interested in the mechanics of how you would actually exploit a connection to the past, which turns out to involve financial derivatives in ways that are both satirically apt and logically necessary.

The prose in the first hundred pages requires patience. Gibson is laying infrastructure. The payoff, when the two timelines become fully comprehensible and start interacting, is considerable.

The Amazon series is competent but simplified. Read the book first.
