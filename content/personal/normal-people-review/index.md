The novel is structured around a series of miscommunications that are not failures of language but failures of nerve. Connell and Marianne understand each other better than almost anyone in their lives. They use that understanding to know exactly when and how to withdraw.

Rooney is precise about class in a way that Irish fiction often is and English fiction usually is not. Connell's discomfort in the Trinity social world is not insecurity in the ordinary sense — it is a specific inability to perform a kind of cultural confidence that was never part of his formation, and the novel never lets him off the hook for using Marianne as a proxy for that confidence.

The second half loses some of the tautness of the first. The European interludes feel like a change of scene chosen for its own sake. But the final chapter is one of the best endings I have read in recent fiction: it resolves nothing, which is exactly right.

I read it in about four hours and thought about it for two weeks.
