---
title: "Against summaries as a reading practice"
publishDate: 2026-02-25
lead: "The summary is a useful tool. As a substitute for reading, it is an epistemic trap."
tags: ["reading", "opinion", "epistemology"]
---

The summary is a useful tool. Used to revisit what you have already read, or to decide whether something is worth reading, it is entirely legitimate. Used as a substitute for reading — as the primary way you encounter a book, a study, or an argument — it is an epistemic trap.

Here is why. An argument is not just a set of conclusions. It is a structure: the order in which premises are introduced, the choice of examples, the qualifications placed on claims, the counter-arguments acknowledged. These structural choices are evidence. They tell you something about the quality of the reasoning, the honesty of the author's engagement with contrary evidence, the confidence that is and is not warranted by the underlying material.

The summary strips most of this out. You get the conclusions and perhaps the key supporting claims. You lose the texture that would let you evaluate them. The result is a confident-sounding belief held without the epistemic basis that produced it.

This problem has been around as long as abstracts and book reviews. It is now significantly worse, because the summary is the default encounter with most knowledge for most people most of the time — and summaries are increasingly being generated by systems whose approach to the texture of an argument is that it is noise to be removed on the way to the point.

I am not arguing that everyone should read everything in full. That is impossible and irrelevant. I am arguing that there is a difference between choosing not to read something and reading the summary while believing you have read the thing. The second is a form of false currency.
