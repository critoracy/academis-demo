## The event

On 21 February 2026, Academis Demo co-hosted a one-day symposium with the Independent Scholars Network on the state of evidential standards in public discourse. Thirteen speakers, thirty-two attendees, one very good morning session, and one afternoon panel that ran significantly over time.

## What was discussed

The morning sessions were organised around three themes:

**Theme 1 — Evidence hierarchies in policy** examined whether the clinical-medicine model of evidence (RCTs at the top, expert opinion at the bottom) translates usefully to policy contexts, and why it sometimes produces worse decisions than more eclectic approaches to evidence synthesis.

**Theme 2 — Journalism and research** addressed the structural incentives that lead to the misrepresentation of scientific findings in general-audience coverage — deadlines, word limits, the absence of specialist knowledge, and the commercial value of counter-intuitive results.

**Theme 3 — Peer review under pressure** considered whether the peer-review system is producing the epistemic goods it is supposed to produce, and what the alternatives might look like.

The afternoon plenary ran an extended argument — by design contentious — that statistical significance has been a net harm to scientific communication. It did not resolve. It was the best part of the day.

## What was not resolved

Several questions were identified as unresolved and worth pursuing:

- Whether pre-registration should be mandatory for publicly funded research
- How to handle the tension between timeliness and rigour in science journalism
- What obligations academic journals have to publish negative results

We intend to commission pieces on the first two of these in the coming months.
