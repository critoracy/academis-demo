## The selection problem

Every piece of evidence you present is a selection from a larger set of possible observations. The argument you hear is shaped as much by what was left out as by what was included — and the same is true of arguments you yourself construct.

This is not an accusation. Selection is unavoidable in any argument of finite length. The question is whether the selection is made honestly, with an awareness of what the excluded evidence would do to the conclusion.

The test of evidential honesty is simple: *could you describe, without being forced, the strongest evidence that runs against your claim?* If you cannot, you do not understand the state of evidence well enough to make a strong claim on the basis of it.

## What makes evidence reliable

Reliable evidence has several identifiable characteristics.

**Independence**: The evidence was produced by a process that did not build in the conclusion. A study funded by an interested party is not automatically unreliable, but it carries a higher burden of independent replication before it should be treated as settled.

**Replicability**: The result has been obtained by independent researchers working from the same or similar data. A single study, however well-conducted, is a data point. A cluster of independent replications is something closer to a finding.

**Pre-registration**: The hypothesis was specified before the data were collected. Post-hoc hypothesis formation — finding a pattern in data and then treating the data as evidence for that pattern — inflates apparent statistical significance.

**Precision and effect size**: A statistically significant result is not necessarily a practically important one. The effect size — how large the measured difference actually is — matters more than the p-value for most policy and practical arguments.

{{< nerdnote title="On p-values" color="info" >}}
A p-value of 0.05 means that, if there were no real effect, you would expect to see a result this extreme roughly one time in twenty by chance. It does not mean the effect has a 95% probability of being real. This distinction is non-trivial; much public misinterpretation of scientific findings rests on conflating the two.
{{< /nerdnote >}}

## When evidence is unavailable

Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence — but it is also not nothing. The inferential value of a negative result depends on whether the absence is the result of adequate search.

If we have looked carefully for evidence of X and not found it, that tells us something. If we have not looked, or have looked in ways that would systematically miss X if it existed, the absence tells us very little.

The honest argumentative move, when evidence is scarce or contested, is to say so explicitly and to calibrate the confidence of the claim accordingly. A well-hedged claim with thin evidence is epistemically stronger than a bold claim that ignores evidential limitations.

## Expert testimony and its appropriate weight

Expert opinion is a form of evidence, but it occupies a specific place in the hierarchy: it is evidence that strong evidence exists, not a substitute for it.

When you cite expert consensus, you are making an argument by authority. This is legitimate under two conditions: the expert has genuine expertise in the relevant domain, and there is genuine consensus rather than merely the opinion of a visible minority.

Both conditions are frequently violated.

- Asserting that a claim is true because an expert said so, in the absence of other evidence

- Using credential asymmetry to shut down legitimate questioning

## Common evidential fallacies

Several argument patterns look like evidence but are not.

**The anecdote as proof**: A single case, however vivid and well-documented, cannot establish a general claim. It can establish that something is possible. It can make an abstract claim concrete and imaginable. It cannot show that the case is representative.

**Cherry-picking**: Presenting a subset of the evidence that supports your conclusion while not disclosing the contradicting evidence. This differs from legitimate selection in that it misrepresents the overall state of the evidence.

**The moving average baseline**: Choosing the starting point of a trend to make the trend look more or less significant than it actually is. Graph the same data starting from different years; the apparent trend changes.

**Precision as confidence**: Presenting a specific number — "productivity increased by 3.7%" — implies a precision that the underlying measurement may not support. Extreme specificity can function as false assurance.

The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool.

## Evaluating evidence you cannot verify directly

In practice, you will rarely be in a position to assess original sources. You are reading summaries, journalism about studies, secondhand accounts of expert opinion. This is normal. It does not make you helpless, but it requires a different kind of vigilance.

Several questions help:

1. **Who produced this evidence, and what were their incentives?** This does not determine reliability, but it shapes what verification effort is appropriate.
2. **What is the publication venue?** Peer review is not a guarantee, but a peer-reviewed journal with a track record is stronger evidence than a press release.
3. **How does this source treat its own limitations?** Research that acknowledges its methodological limits is more trustworthy than research that does not, because the former at least indicates that the authors thought about them.
4. **Have I been able to find anything that contradicts this?** A quick search for "X study criticism" or "X finding replication" often surfaces the key objections.

## What to do with weak evidence

The right response to weak evidence is not to discard it but to use it at the weight it deserves. Weak evidence for a claim, combined with weak evidence for an alternative explanation, often still permits a tentative conclusion — one held provisionally and liable to revision.

What is not legitimate is to use weak evidence as though it were strong, or to present a conclusion confidently on the basis of evidence that you know is limited. The argument from epistemic humility is not a retreat — it is a stronger position than overstatement, because it is less vulnerable to falsification.

In Chapter 3, we examine a different kind of argumentative weakness: not the misuse of evidence but the deployment of fallacious reasoning patterns that make a bad argument look like a good one.
