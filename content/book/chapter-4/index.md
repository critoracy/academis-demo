## What steelmanning is

Steelmanning is the practice of identifying and engaging with the strongest version of an opposing argument — as opposed to the weakest version, which is what *strawmanning* addresses. It is the opposite of the tendency to represent an opponent's position in the most easily defeated form.

The term is not universally used, and the concept is simple enough not to require it. The question is always: *if a thoughtful, well-informed person held the opposite view, what would their best argument be?*

This matters for two reasons. First, an argument that survives only against weak objections is not a strong argument — it is an argument that has not yet been tested. Second, genuinely engaging with strong counter-arguments often produces a better final position than the one you started with, because the process of taking objections seriously tends to reveal genuine weaknesses in your premises and genuine strengths in the opposing view.

## The mechanics of a counter-argument

A counter-argument is not a restatement of disagreement. It has the same structural requirements as an argument: a claim, evidence, and reasoning.

A counter-argument can target any component of your original argument:

- **Against the claim**: asserting that the conclusion is false, with supporting evidence
- **Against the evidence**: contesting the quality, relevance, or interpretation of your evidence
- **Against the reasoning**: accepting the evidence but disputing that it supports the conclusion you draw from it

Effective response to a counter-argument requires identifying which component it targets. A rebuttal to an evidential objection is different from a rebuttal to a reasoning objection, and conflating them usually produces a confused response that neither concedes gracefully nor defends confidently.

{{< box variant="highlight" >}}
**The three-step rebuttal:**
1. Identify precisely what the counter-argument is targeting (claim, evidence, or reasoning)
2. State the strongest version of the objection before you respond to it
3. Either concede the point (if it is valid) or explain specifically why it does not undermine your conclusion
{{< /box >}}

## When to concede

Concession is not defeat. A well-placed concession is one of the most powerful moves in substantive argumentation, because it signals that you are reasoning rather than advocating.

There are two types of concession worth distinguishing:

**Concede and rebut**: "This is a valid observation, but it does not undermine the main claim because…" This works when the counter-argument identifies a genuine limitation of your evidence or a specific exception to your claim, but the exception does not invalidate the claim at the level of generality at which you are making it.

**Full concession**: acknowledging that the counter-argument reveals a genuine flaw in your position. This is rare in adversarial settings (formal debate, political argument) but should be more common in collaborative intellectual settings. The intellectual progress in a conversation is often fastest precisely at the point when one party says: "I hadn't thought of that, and you're right."

A position that is never revised in response to objections is a position that is not really open to argument. Willingness to update is not a sign of weakness — it is the operating condition of honest intellectual engagement.

## The strongest objections to common arguments

Knowing the strongest objections to common argument types in advance is useful preparation. A few examples from the themes in this guide:

{{< cols left="50" right="50" >}}
{{< col >}}
**If you argue from empirical evidence:**

The strongest objection is usually about confounding variables or selection effects. Anticipate it: which alternative explanations could account for your finding, and why do you rule them out?

**If you argue from expert consensus:**

The strongest objection is that the apparent consensus is narrower or more contested than you represent. Anticipate it: what do genuine dissenters in the field say, and why does the consensus outweigh them?
{{< /col >}}
{{< col >}}
**If you argue from principle:**

The strongest objection is usually a limiting case — a situation in which applying your principle produces a result that seems clearly wrong. Anticipate it: what are the limiting cases, and do you accept the implications or modify the principle to exclude them?

**If you argue from analogy:**

The strongest objection targets the disanalogies — the ways in which the compared cases differ in a way that undermines the conclusion. Anticipate it: in what ways are the cases different, and do those differences matter?
{{< /col >}}
{{< /cols >}}

## The structural position of counter-arguments

In a written argument, where to place counter-argument and rebuttal is partly a tactical and partly an ethical question.

**Front-loading** objections — raising them before the reader raises them internally — signals intellectual confidence and honesty. It prevents the reader from finishing the essay thinking "but what about X?" and suspecting that you avoided X deliberately.

**Late placement** is appropriate when the counter-argument requires significant background to understand, and that background is best built through the main argument first.

**Omission** is appropriate for genuinely weak objections that are not in legitimate circulation — objections that no thoughtful person would seriously hold. For any objection that a well-informed sceptic might raise, omitting it reads as evasion.

{{< pull-quote autor="John Stuart Mill" >}}
He who knows only his own side of the case knows little of that. His reasons may be good, and no one may have been able to refute them. But if he is equally unable to refute the reasons on the opposite side; if he does not so much as know what they are, he has no ground for preferring either opinion.
{{< /pull-quote >}}

## Disagreement that is not resolvable

Not every disagreement is a matter of one party having better evidence or better reasoning. Some disagreements persist between people who are well-informed and reasoning well, because they differ on foundational values or on how to weigh competing considerations.

These disagreements are not resolvable by argument in the usual sense. What argument can do is clarify *where* the disagreement actually lies. If a political disagreement looks like a factual dispute but turns out to be a values dispute in disguise, the conversation becomes much more tractable once the disguise is removed. Identifying that two people agree on the facts but disagree on which value should take priority is itself a significant intellectual achievement.

The final chapter addresses the craft of translating all of this into written prose — from notes and structure to a finished argument that communicates clearly to a reader who is not already convinced.
