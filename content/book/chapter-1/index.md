## The three-part structure

An argument is not a fight. In the sense used throughout this guide, an argument is a structured attempt to justify a claim — to give someone good reason to believe something on the basis of evidence and reasoning rather than authority, repetition, or social pressure.

Every argument has three structural components:

**The claim** is the position you are defending. It is the answer to the question: *what are you asserting?* A claim must be specific enough to be falsifiable — if there is no possible evidence that could show it to be wrong, it is not a claim but a definition, a tautology, or an aspiration.

**The evidence** is the information you offer in support of the claim. Evidence can take many forms: statistical data, documented cases, experimental results, testimony from credible observers, logical entailments from accepted principles. Not all evidence is equally strong, and a central skill of argumentation is assessing the quality of evidence rather than merely its presence.

**The reasoning** is the logical link between the evidence and the claim — the explanation of *why* the evidence supports the claim rather than something else. This is the element most frequently omitted, and its absence is the source of most of the weak arguments you will encounter.

{{< nerdnote title="Common mistake" color="warning" >}}
Stating a claim and presenting evidence is not enough. Without explicit reasoning, you are asking your audience to supply the inferential link themselves — and they may not supply the one you intended. Make the reasoning explicit, every time.
{{< /nerdnote >}}

## Claims in more depth

Claims vary in logical character. Distinguishing the type of claim you are making shapes what kind of evidence is relevant.

**Empirical claims** assert something about how the world is — a matter of fact that could, in principle, be confirmed or disconfirmed by observation or data. *"Road fatalities fell by 12% in the five years following the speed limit change"* is an empirical claim. It requires empirical evidence.

**Causal claims** are a subset of empirical claims that specifically assert a relationship of cause and effect. They require stronger evidential standards than mere correlation. *"The speed limit change caused the reduction in road fatalities"* cannot be established by the same evidence that establishes the first claim; it requires ruling out alternative explanations.

**Normative claims** assert what ought to be — what is good, right, justified, or preferable. They cannot be established by empirical evidence alone, though empirical evidence is often relevant to them. *"The speed limit should be reduced on residential roads"* is a normative claim. It can be supported by empirical evidence about safety outcomes, but it also involves a prior commitment to the value of preventing road deaths, which is itself a normative premise that can be questioned.

**Definitional claims** assert that something fits or does not fit a category. They are often disguised as empirical or normative claims. Whether a particular act constitutes "censorship" or a particular policy constitutes "discrimination" frequently hinges on definitional questions that are worth extracting and arguing about on their own terms.

{{< pull-quote autor="A. J. Ayer" >}}
The meaning of a proposition is the method of its verification.
{{< /pull-quote >}}

## Evidence and its hierarchy

Not all evidence is created equal. A useful heuristic — borrowed from clinical medicine and adapted here — runs roughly as follows, from stronger to weaker:

1. **Systematic reviews and meta-analyses** of multiple independent studies, when the domain permits them
2. **Controlled experimental evidence** — randomised trials, natural experiments, difference-in-differences analyses
3. **Observational evidence** from large, well-documented datasets
4. **Case studies** — detailed but not necessarily representative
5. **Testimony and expert opinion** — valuable as a pointer but not as a substitute for direct evidence
6. **Anecdote** — the lowest evidential tier, though not zero; a single vivid case can establish possibility

The hierarchy is a heuristic, not a law. Context determines what evidence is available, and an honest acknowledgement of evidential limitations is itself a mark of a good argument.

## Reasoning: the bridge

Reasoning is the logical connection between evidence and claim. The question it answers is: *how does this evidence establish this conclusion?*

There are two main forms of reasoning in ordinary argumentation:

**Deductive reasoning** moves from general premises to specific conclusions. If the premises are true and the argument is valid, the conclusion follows necessarily. The problem is that valid deductive arguments require premises that are themselves established, which usually involves inductive reasoning at some prior stage.

**Inductive reasoning** moves from specific observations to general conclusions. It cannot produce certainty, but it can produce justified degrees of belief. The strength of inductive reasoning depends on the size and representativeness of the evidential base, the absence of confounding factors, and the plausibility of alternative explanations.

Most real arguments combine both. Being clear about which type of reasoning you are deploying — and acknowledging its limitations — is a significant part of argumentative honesty.

## Putting it together: a worked example

Consider the claim: *"Requiring university applicants to submit personal statements disadvantages students from state schools."*

- **Claim type**: empirical-causal
- **What it requires**: evidence that students from state schools perform less well under personal-statement systems than under systems without them; and reasoning that rules out the alternative explanation that state-school students simply have weaker applications for other reasons

A weak argument presents statistics showing that state-school applicants are under-represented at elite universities and concludes that personal statements are to blame. The evidence is real; the reasoning is not. The same outcome is consistent with many other explanations.

A stronger argument would compare admission rates across universities that weight personal statements differently, control for other variables, and address the confounders directly.

{{< box variant="highlight" >}}
**Summary: the self-check**

Before you present an argument, ask:
1. Have I stated my claim precisely enough that someone could tell me what would count as evidence against it?
2. Is my evidence strong enough for the type of claim I am making?
3. Have I made explicit *why* the evidence supports the claim rather than an alternative conclusion?

If you cannot answer yes to all three, the argument is not ready.
{{< /box >}}
