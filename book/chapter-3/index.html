A Field Guide to Good Arguments

Chapter 3: Fallacies and Misdirection

A fallacy is a reasoning error that makes an invalid argument look valid. Learning to recognise them is not about catching people out — it is about protecting your own thinking from patterns that feel compelling but do not actually work.

Elena Marsh

Why fallacies persist

Fallacious arguments are not confined to bad faith or low intelligence. They persist because they work — at least in the short term, at least with audiences who are not actively looking for them. Many fallacies exploit genuine cognitive shortcuts that are adaptive in other contexts. The pattern-matching that makes ad hominem feel like good reasoning is the same pattern-matching that correctly tells us to distrust someone who has lied to us before.

The goal of this chapter is not to produce people who reflexively shout fallacy names at arguments they dislike. That is itself a kind of misdirection. The goal is to identify the structural error in a piece of reasoning clearly enough to explain what a better argument would look like.

Fallacies of relevance

These errors introduce considerations that do not actually bear on the truth of the claim.

Ad hominem (against the person): attacking the arguer rather than the argument. The motivation, character, or interests of the person making a claim do not determine whether the claim is true. A corrupt politician can be correct about macroeconomic policy. A disreputable scientist can have produced valid experimental results.

The legitimate cousin of ad hominem is the argument from conflict of interest: the observation that someone’s incentives give them reason to misrepresent evidence. This is not an ad hominem because it is relevant — it is a reason to scrutinise the evidence more carefully, not to dismiss the claim without examination.

Appeal to authority (misused): as discussed in Chapter 2, authority is evidence, but it is not decisive evidence and it must be used carefully. Citing a credential where a citation to evidence is needed is a substitution fallacy.

Appeal to popularity: the fact that many people believe something does not make it true. Consensus among genuine experts in a relevant domain is evidential; popularity among the general public is not.

Red herring: introducing a consideration that is tangentially related but does not address the actual argument. “You criticise our procurement process, but what about the failures under the previous government?” may or may not be a useful point, but it is not a response to the argument about the current procurement process.

The genetic fallacy

A specific form of ad hominem worth naming: the genetic fallacy dismisses an argument on the grounds of where it came from rather than its logical content. “That idea originated with the Nazis” does not tell you whether the idea is correct; many true things have been believed by terrible people. Evaluating an idea requires engaging with the idea, not its genealogy.

Fallacies of presumption

These errors embed hidden assumptions that are doing the argumentative work without being acknowledged.

Begging the question (petitio principii): the conclusion is hidden in the premises. “The current immigration policy is wrong because it goes against our values” presumes that our values are correctly characterised and that they oppose the policy — the very things in dispute.

False dilemma: presenting two options as though they are exhaustive when they are not. “Either we restrict speech online, or we accept uncontrolled disinformation” ignores the large space of intermediate and alternative options. False dilemmas are particularly common in policy debates, where the rhetorical pressure to choose a side is highest.

Loaded question: formulating a question so that any direct answer accepts a contested premise. “Have you stopped exaggerating your research findings?” cannot be answered with a simple yes or no without accepting the premise that you did exaggerate them.

Slippery slope: asserting that a moderate step will inevitably lead to an extreme outcome, without establishing the mechanism that makes each step in the sequence necessary. Some slippery slope arguments are legitimate — if there are real mechanisms that would make the sequence follow — and some are not. The distinguishing question is: what is the mechanism, and how robust is it?

Fallacies of ambiguity

These errors depend on terms shifting meaning between different parts of an argument.

Equivocation: using the same word with different meanings in different parts of the argument. “Laws of nature cannot be violated; the laws of the state are laws; therefore the laws of the state cannot be violated.” The word laws is doing two incompatible jobs here.

Amphiboly: an argument whose force depends on grammatical or syntactic ambiguity rather than logical content.

Composition and division: assuming that what is true of the parts is true of the whole (composition), or vice versa. “Each player on the team is talented; therefore the team is talented” fails in practice because team performance depends on coordination, not merely the aggregate of individual skills.

Fallacies of causation

These errors involve misidentifying or mischaracterising causal relationships.

Post hoc ergo propter hoc (after this, therefore because of this): inferring causation from temporal sequence. B followed A, therefore A caused B. This ignores coincidence, confounding variables, reverse causation, and the possibility that both A and B are caused by a common prior factor.

Correlation as causation: the observation that two things vary together does not establish which causes which, or whether either causes the other. Ice cream sales and drowning rates are positively correlated; removing ice cream from sale would not reduce drownings.

Counterfactual inflation: claiming credit for preventing an outcome that may not have occurred in any case. “Our intervention prevented a recession” is unfalsifiable if no recession occurred; it depends entirely on what would have happened otherwise, which cannot be directly observed.

The most seductive argument is one that is almost right — close enough to the truth that the error is not immediately visible, but wrong in a way that matters for the conclusion.

Using this in practice

The most useful application of this chapter is not labelling fallacies as a rhetorical move — “that’s an ad hominem, therefore wrong” — but asking what a valid version of the same argument would look like.

If someone is making an argument from authority, ask: what is the relevant evidence the authority is pointing to? If the argument is a slippery slope, ask: what is the specific mechanism claimed? If the argument appears to beg the question, ask: what would the argument look like with the contested premise stated explicitly and defended?

Reconstructing the strongest version of an argument, and then engaging with that version, is more intellectually productive than identifying the weakest version and dismissing it. It is also more likely to produce genuine understanding.
