A Field Guide to Good Arguments

Chapter 5: Writing the Argument

The argument exists in your head before it exists on the page, and the two versions are rarely identical. Writing is not the transcription of finished thinking — it is a stage in the development of the thinking itself.

Elena Marsh

Why writing changes the argument

Before you write an argument, you have a mental sketch — a rough sense of the claim, a cluster of supporting ideas, some intuition about the objections. This sketch feels more complete than it is, because your mind fills in the gaps automatically as you think.

Writing forces the gaps into the open. The sentence that refuses to complete itself, the paragraph that keeps doubling back, the transition that sounds reasonable out loud but reads as a non sequitur on the page — these are not stylistic failures. They are diagnostic signals: the point at which the argument has not yet resolved itself.

This means the first draft of an argument is not a polished version of a finished thought. It is the instrument through which you find out whether you actually have a finished thought. Write the first draft to discover the argument, not to communicate it.

Structure before prose

The most common mistake in argumentative writing is starting to write before the structure is clear.

Structure is the logical skeleton of the argument: the sequence of claims, the placement of evidence, the position of counter-arguments, the path from introduction to conclusion. It is best worked out on paper, not in running prose.

A minimal structure document for an argument of essay length looks like this:

  1. Opening: what problem or question is this addressing, and why does it matter?
  2. Claim: what is the central thesis?
  3. The strongest evidence, in the most useful order
  4. The main objections, and the responses to them
  5. Conclusion: what follows from having established the claim?

The order of (3) and (4) is a matter of judgement, as discussed in the previous chapter. The important thing is that both are in the plan before you write a sentence of prose.

The one-paragraph test

If you cannot summarise your argument in a single paragraph — claim, two or three key pieces of supporting evidence, and the main thing you are conceding — the argument is not yet ready to write at length. The paragraph does not need to be polished; it needs to be complete. You should be able to show it to someone and have them understand what you are arguing and on what grounds.

The opening

The opening has one job: to make the reader want to continue.

It does not need to be dramatic. What it needs is a clear signal of what the argument is about, why it matters, and what you are going to claim. An opening that is too broad (“Throughout human history, people have debated…”) delays all of this without adding anything. An opening that is too narrow loses readers who do not yet know why the specific question matters.

The most reliable structure for an argumentative opening is: state the problem or question, explain why it is consequential or unresolved, and indicate the direction of the answer you will offer. This can be done in a few sentences. Longer openings are not more impressive; they are more likely to lose readers before the argument begins.

An essay that begins by announcing what it is going to say, says it, and then announces what it just said, has three times as many words as it needs.

Paragraph structure

Each paragraph should do a single thing. The first sentence should state what that thing is — not as a topic heading, but as a substantive claim or observation that the rest of the paragraph develops.

The body of the paragraph presents evidence, example, or reasoning that establishes or illustrates the opening claim. The closing sentence either summarises the point or transitions to the next one.

Paragraphs that wander — that contain two or three distinct points loosely connected — are the most common source of argumentative confusion in otherwise competent writing. When a paragraph does not hold together, the question to ask is: is this one thought or two? If it is two, split it. If it is one, cut everything that belongs to the other thought.

The evidence-reasoning-conclusion pattern

At the paragraph level, the argument structure from Chapter 1 maps directly onto prose. A paragraph that makes an evidential point well typically follows this pattern:

  1. State the claim the evidence will support
  2. Present the evidence (citation, data, example)
  3. Explain the reasoning — why does this evidence support the claim?
  4. Acknowledge the limit — what would this evidence not establish?

The fourth step is omitted in most argumentative writing, and its omission is almost always a mistake. Readers who notice what the evidence does not establish will assume you did not notice, or that you are hiding it.

Transitions and logical connectives

The logical relationships between paragraphs and sections must be made explicit. “Furthermore,” “however,” “this does not establish,” “the stronger objection is,” “the exception is instructive” — these connectives tell the reader what kind of move the argument is making.

Transitions that are too vague (“Moreover,” “In addition”) repeat the motion of moving forward without specifying the logical direction. Prefer connectives that name the relationship: this strengthens the previous point, this is a parallel case, this is the exception that tests the rule, this is the objection that follows naturally from what was just conceded.

**A note on hedging:** Appropriate hedging — "this suggests," "the evidence is consistent with," "a plausible interpretation" — is a sign of intellectual precision, not weakness. It calibrates confidence to evidence. Inappropriate hedging — deploying it everywhere to avoid committing to any claim — evacuates the argument of content. The test: are you hedging because the evidence genuinely does not permit more certainty, or because you want to avoid being held accountable for the claim?

The conclusion

A conclusion should do three things: recall the main claim (without restating the introduction), summarise what has been established rather than merely asserted, and indicate what follows from it — what the reader should now believe or do differently.

What it should not do is introduce new evidence, raise new objections, or expand into territory the argument has not prepared the ground for. A conclusion that suddenly broadens into large generalisations has not concluded — it has started a new argument and abandoned the one it was supposed to finish.

Revision

Argumentative writing is improved more by structural revision — moving things, cutting sections, changing the sequence of claims — than by line-level editing. Most writers revise in the wrong direction: polishing prose before the structure is right. This produces beautiful sentences in the wrong order.

The most useful question in revision is: does this paragraph need to be here, and is this the right place for it? If the answer to either part is no, no amount of sentence-level improvement will save it.

The second most useful question is: at every point where I move from one idea to the next, is the logical relationship between them explicit?

This concludes the guide. The five chapters have covered the structure of arguments, the assessment of evidence, the recognition of fallacies, the engagement with counter-arguments, and the craft of writing arguments in prose. The best way to continue is to write — and to find someone who will tell you, specifically, where your reasoning fails.
